Introduction to Problem Solving

1. Why This Section Exists

Most coding interviews do not test whether you can recall algorithms or data structures from memory.

They test whether you can:

  • understand an unfamiliar problem,
  • reason about constraints,
  • choose an appropriate approach,
  • and write correct, explainable code under time pressure.

This section exists to train that exact skill ‚Äî the ability to think clearly when the solution is not immediately obvious.

2. What Interviewers Are Actually Evaluating

During a live coding round (CoderPad, whiteboard, or shared editor), interviewers are not silently judging syntax.

They are actively observing:

  • how you interpret the problem statement
  • whether you ask clarifying questions
  • how you reason about constraints and edge cases
  • how you respond when you are unsure
  • how clearly you communicate while coding

The final solution matters ‚Äî but the thinking process matters more.

A candidate who thinks clearly and communicates well is often preferred over one who reaches a perfect solution silently.

3. Knowledge vs Application

There is a clear separation of concerns across learning areas:

  • Data Structures ‚Üí what tools exist
  • Algorithms ‚Üí how problems are solved in theory
  • Problem Solving ‚Üí how those tools are applied under real constraints

Many candidates know data structures and algorithms in isolation, yet struggle in interviews because they cannot connect them when the problem is unfamiliar.

This section bridges that gap.

4. What Problem Solving Really Means

Problem solving is the ability to:

  • translate a problem statement into a solvable model,
  • identify what truly needs optimization (time, space, clarity),
  • and progressively refine a solution.

It is not about arriving at the optimal solution instantly.

It is about making steady, logical progress ‚Äî and explaining your decisions as you go.

This is exactly what interviews are designed to surface.

5. Real Interview Constraints

Most interviews introduce at least one of the following challenges:

  • ambiguous or incomplete requirements
  • strict time limits
  • unfamiliar problem framing
  • follow-up questions that change constraints

This section prepares you to remain structured, calm, and effective in those situations ‚Äî even when the problem feels uncomfortable at first.

6. Scope of This Section

This Problem Solving section focuses on:

  • thinking frameworks
  • reusable problem patterns
  • execution discipline
  • communication during coding

It intentionally avoids:

  • teaching data structure internals
  • algorithm theory
  • design principles (covered in LLD)

Those topics are assumed knowledge and referenced only when needed.

7. Expected Outcome

After completing this section, you should be able to:

  • approach unseen problems methodically,
  • recognize common patterns quickly,
  • write clean, runnable code in live environments,
  • and explain your reasoning confidently.

This skill directly applies to:

  • CoderPad rounds
  • whiteboard interviews
  • real-world debugging
